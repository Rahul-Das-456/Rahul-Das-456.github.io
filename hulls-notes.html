Forwards, futures, margin, pricing, and market mechanics from John C. Hull's Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives, 11th ed. Click any question to expand.

Both are agreements to buy or sell an asset at a future date for a price locked in today. Same economic idea. Different wrappers.

A forward is a private deal between two parties. Negotiated directly, fully customizable: any size, any date, any underlying. Trades OTC with no exchange and no clearinghouse. You are exposed to the other side.

A future is the standardized version on an exchange: CME, ICE, CBOE. The exchange sets contract size and delivery dates. No customization. What you gain is liquidity and no counterparty risk because the clearinghouse steps between both sides.

Forwards settle once at maturity. Futures are marked to market every single day. Gains and losses hit your margin account overnight.

Both forwards and futures have the same payoff shape. Perfectly linear. No kinks, no caps, no floors.

Long (agreed to buy at F_0): Short (agreed to sell at F_0): P&L P&L | / | \ | / + | \ + | / | \ | / ------|---------\-----> S_T ---|---------/----------> S_T | F_0 \ | / F_0 - | \ - | / | \ | /
Long: P&L = S_T - F_0 Short: P&L = F_0 - S_T

S_T is the spot price at delivery. F_0 is the locked-in price. No premium paid upfront. Zero cost to enter. Both sides are fully obligated. That is what separates this from options.

One word: counterparty risk.

You locked in a price with another party months ago. Delivery day comes. The market moved hard against them. They do not show up. No exchange, no clearinghouse, nobody guaranteeing the other side. You are holding a contract and the counterparty is bankrupt or walking away.

The forward is only as good as the credit of the person on the other side. Institutions run credit checks, use ISDA master agreements, and maintain credit departments to manage this exposure across thousands of bilateral contracts.

Two other risks. Liquidity risk: no exchange to exit early, you negotiate a termination or find someone to take it off you. Operational risk: bilateral, customized contracts have more room for settlement errors.

Futures eliminate counterparty risk by design. You lose customization. You gain certainty of settlement.

The clearinghouse sits in the middle of every futures trade. The moment your trade executes, it becomes the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer. You no longer have a contract with the person on the other side. You have a contract with the clearinghouse.

Counterparty risk does not disappear. It gets centralized onto one entity built to handle it. Three mechanisms keep it solvent.

Margin requirements. Every participant posts initial margin before trading. First line of defense if someone cannot pay.

Mark to market. Losses are collected daily. By delivery day, almost all gains and losses have already settled in cash. No giant lump sum exposure sitting on the books.

Default fund. Every clearing member contributes to a pooled fund. If one member blows through their margin, the fund absorbs it. The clearinghouse's own capital is the last resort.

2008 context

Lehman had massive OTC derivatives exposure with no clearinghouse. When they failed, nobody knew who was exposed to whom. Exchange-cleared futures kept functioning normally through the same period. Dodd-Frank 2010 pushed large portions of OTC derivatives into central clearing because of exactly this.

At expiry, no. The diagrams are identical. Same formula, same linear shape, same breakeven at F_0.

The theoretical difference comes from daily settlement. If asset prices are positively correlated with interest rates, futures prices will be slightly higher than forward prices. When the asset rises you collect daily gains and reinvest at higher rates. When it falls you pay losses when rates are lower. That asymmetry gets priced in. For most assets and time horizons the difference is negligible.

The meaningful practical difference is path dependency. A forward does not care what happens between today and maturity. A futures position gets margin-called along the way. You can be right on direction, have the price crash and recover, and still get wiped out if you could not fund the margin calls during the drawdown. Same final payoff on paper. Completely different experience getting there.

Margin is a good-faith deposit you post to open a futures position. Not a fee, not a down payment. Pure collateral.

Initial margin is what you post to open, typically 5-10% of notional. Maintenance margin is the floor, usually around 75% of initial. Variation margin is the daily cash that flows in and out as the position is marked to market.

Gold futures: F_0 = $1,750/oz | Contract = 100 oz | Notional = $175,000 Initial margin = $6,000 | Maintenance = $4,500

Day 1: you post $6,000, you are long. Day 2: gold drops $25, you lose $2,500 (25 x 100), account goes to $3,500. Still above maintenance. Day 3: gold drops $15 more, account hits $2,000. Below $4,500 floor. Margin call.

The call requires you to top back up to the initial margin level, not maintenance. Wire $4,000 by end of day or the position gets liquidated at market.

What people get wrong

You can be right on direction and still lose. Gold drops, triggers your margin call, you cannot fund it in time, you get liquidated. Then gold recovers. You called the trade correctly and lost money because you could not survive the path.

The cost of carry model. The futures price is what it costs to buy the asset today and hold it to delivery.

F_0 = S_0 * e^(rT) S_0 = spot price today r = risk-free rate T = time to delivery in years

Arbitrage locks the price in. If futures trade above this, you borrow cash, buy spot, sell the futures, deliver at expiry, pocket the spread risk-free. If futures trade below, you reverse it. Free money closes the gap instantly.

Dividend-paying stock: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r - q)T) [q = dividend yield] Foreign currency: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r - rf)T) [rf = foreign rate] Commodity with storage: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r + u)T) [u = storage cost] Commodity with yield: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r + u - y)T) [y = convenience yield]

The intuition is always the same: futures price equals spot price plus net cost of carry to delivery.

The benefit of physically holding a commodity that you cannot get from holding a futures contract.

You own a refinery. You need crude oil on hand to keep production running. If supply gets disrupted tomorrow, you cannot feed your refinery with a paper contract. Physical inventory has operational value beyond the financial exposure. That extra value is the convenience yield.

F_0 = S_0 * e^((r + u - y)T) y reduces the futures price. Higher y = lower F_0 relative to spot.

When convenience yield is high, people really want the physical commodity right now. They bid up spot. Futures stays lower because the operational benefit does not transfer to a paper contract.

You cannot observe it directly. Back it out from market prices using the pricing equation. It matters most for oil, natural gas, and agricultural products. Financial assets have no convenience yield. High convenience yield also drives backwardation.

These describe the shape of the futures curve: futures prices plotted across different delivery dates.

Contango is the normal state. Futures are higher than spot, longer-dated contracts cost more than shorter-dated ones. Curve slopes upward. Cost of carry doing its job: financing and storage pile up the further out you go.

Contango: Backwardation: Price Price | * | * (spot) | * | * | * | * | * (spot) | * |____________________> |____________________> Time to delivery Time to delivery

Backwardation is when futures are below spot. Near-dated contracts trade higher than far-dated ones. Curve slopes downward. Happens when convenience yield overwhelms cost of carry. Oil during a supply shock. Natural gas heading into winter with low inventory. The market is saying the asset is more valuable in your hands today than in a warehouse for future delivery.

Roll yield. In contango you sell the expiring contract cheap and buy the next one expensive on every roll. You bleed. In backwardation you collect on every roll. This is why long-term commodity ETFs holding futures can massively underperform spot price in sustained contango.

Market signal. Steep backwardation signals tight current supply or high near-term demand. Steep contango can signal oversupply or weak near-term demand with inventory building.

To reduce uncertainty around cash flows. Not to make money. To remove a financial variable they cannot control so they can focus on the business they actually run.

Airline. Buys jet fuel constantly. Oil spikes 40%, cost base blows up. They buy oil futures to lock in fuel costs months in advance. Southwest did this well for years and had a structural cost advantage over competitors who did not.

US exporter. Gets paid in euros, converts back to dollars. Dollar strengthens, real revenue falls even though the sale price did not change. They sell EUR/USD forwards to lock in the exchange rate today.

Floating rate borrower. Exposed to rising rates on their debt. They enter an interest rate swap: pay fixed, receive floating. Converts the exposure to a fixed cost.

Hull's key point

Hedging does not guarantee a better outcome than not hedging. If the airline hedges oil at $90 and oil drops to $60, the hedge hurt them ex-post. That is not the point. The point is removing uncertainty so management can plan and operate without a commodity bet sitting inside the business.

Instructions to the exchange controlling exactly how your trade executes. The price you get depends heavily on which one you use.

Market Order
Execute immediately at best available price. Guaranteed fill, no price control. Slippage risk in thin or fast-moving markets.
Limit Order
Execute only at your specified price or better. Price control, no fill guarantee. Sits in the order book until hit or cancelled.
Stop Order
Dormant until price hits your trigger, then converts to a market order. Used to cut losses. Risk: in a fast market you fill well past your trigger.
Stop-Limit Order
Converts to a limit order at trigger. More price control, less fill certainty. If the market gaps through your limit you do not get filled.
Market-if-Touched (MIT)
Opposite of a stop. Triggers at your level and fires a market order. Used to enter on pullbacks or take profits at a target. Hull mentions this for futures markets specifically.
Day Order
Expires at end of session if not filled. Default for most orders.
Good-till-Cancelled (GTC)
Stays live until you cancel it or it fills. Can sit in the book for weeks.
Fill-or-Kill (FOK)
Fill immediately and in full or cancel entirely. Used by institutions where partial fills create more problems than no fill.
