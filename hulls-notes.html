Section 1 — Forwards and Futures
Ch. 1-5  ·  11 questions

Both are agreements to buy or sell an asset at a future date for a price locked in today. Same economic idea. Different wrappers.

A forward is a private OTC deal. Fully customizable: any size, any date, any underlying. No exchange, no clearinghouse. You are exposed to the other side.

A future is the standardized exchange-traded version (CME, ICE, CBOE). No customization. The clearinghouse steps between both sides and eliminates counterparty risk.

Forwards settle once at maturity. Futures are marked to market every day. Gains and losses hit your margin account overnight.

Perfectly linear. No kinks, no caps, no floors. Same shape for both forwards and futures.

Long (buy at F_0): Short (sell at F_0): P&L P&L | / | \ + | / + | \ | / | \ ---|---------/------> S_T ------|---------\----> S_T | / F_0 | F_0 \ - | / - | \
Long: P&L = S_T - F_0 Short: P&L = F_0 - S_T

No premium paid upfront. Zero cost to enter. Both sides fully obligated.

Counterparty risk. Delivery day comes, the market moved against the other side, and they do not show up. No exchange, no clearinghouse, nobody guaranteeing settlement.

Two additional risks: liquidity risk (no exchange to exit early, you negotiate a termination) and operational risk (bilateral customized contracts have more room for errors).

Futures eliminate counterparty risk. You trade customization for certainty of settlement.

The clearinghouse sits between every futures trade. Once your trade executes, it becomes the buyer to every seller and seller to every buyer. Counterparty risk is centralized onto one entity built to handle it.

Three mechanisms: margin requirements (collateral posted upfront), mark to market (losses collected daily, no giant end-of-term exposure), default fund (pooled contributions from clearing members absorb defaults before the clearinghouse's own capital is touched).

2008 context

Lehman had massive bilateral OTC exposure with no clearinghouse. When they failed nobody knew who owed what to whom. Exchange-cleared futures kept functioning normally through the same period. Dodd-Frank 2010 pushed large portions of OTC derivatives into central clearing because of this.

At expiry, none. Identical formula, identical shape, identical breakeven at F_0.

Theoretical difference: if asset prices are positively correlated with interest rates, futures prices will be slightly higher than forward prices. Daily gains get reinvested at higher rates; daily losses are paid when rates are lower. The asymmetry gets priced in. Negligible for most assets.

Practical difference: path dependency. A forward ignores what happens between today and maturity. Futures get margin-called along the way. You can call the direction right and still get wiped out if you could not survive the drawdown to your margin floor.

Margin is a good-faith deposit to open a futures position. Not a fee, not a down payment. Pure collateral.

Initial margin: what you post to open (5-10% of notional). Maintenance margin: the floor (~75% of initial). Variation margin: daily cash that flows in and out via mark to market.

Gold: F_0=$1,750, contract=100oz, notional=$175,000 Initial=$6,000 | Maintenance=$4,500 Day 2: gold drops $25 -> lose $2,500 -> account $3,500 (ok) Day 3: gold drops $15 -> lose $1,500 -> account $2,000 (MARGIN CALL) Must top back up to $6,000 (initial), not $4,500 (maintenance). Wire $4,000 by EOD or position gets liquidated at market.
What people get wrong

You can nail the direction and still lose. Get margin-called at the bottom, cannot fund it, get liquidated. Then the asset recovers. Being right is not enough if you cannot survive the path.

The cost of carry model. Futures price = spot price plus net cost of carrying the asset to delivery. Arbitrage locks it in.

Base: F_0 = S_0 * e^(rT) Dividend-paying stock: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r-q)T) [q = dividend yield] Foreign currency: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r-rf)T) [rf = foreign rate] Commodity with storage: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r+u)T) [u = storage cost] With convenience yield: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r+u-y)T) [y = convenience yield]

If futures trade above the formula, you borrow cash, buy spot, sell futures, deliver at expiry, and pocket the spread risk-free. If too cheap, reverse it. Free money closes the gap instantly.

The benefit of physically holding a commodity that you cannot get from holding a futures contract. You own a refinery, you need crude on hand. If supply gets disrupted tomorrow, a paper contract cannot feed your refinery. That operational value is the convenience yield.

It reduces the futures price. Higher convenience yield means lower F_0 relative to spot. When y is high, people bid up spot. Futures stays lower because the benefit does not transfer to paper.

You cannot observe it directly. Back it out from market prices using the pricing equation. Matters most for oil, gas, and agricultural products. Financial assets have no convenience yield. High convenience yield drives backwardation.

Contango: futures above spot, longer-dated contracts more expensive than shorter-dated. Curve slopes upward. Normal state. Cost of carry doing its job.

Backwardation: futures below spot, near-dated contracts more expensive than far-dated. Curve slopes downward. Convenience yield overwhelms cost of carry. Market says the asset is more valuable in your hands today than in storage for future delivery.

Contango: Backwardation: Price Price | * | * (spot) | * | * | * | * | * (spot) | * |____________________> |____________________> Time to delivery Time to delivery

Roll yield: in contango you sell cheap and buy expensive on every roll, bleeding steadily. In backwardation you collect on every roll. This is why commodity ETFs holding futures can massively underperform spot in sustained contango.

To reduce uncertainty around cash flows. Not to make money. To remove financial variables they cannot control so they can focus on the business they actually run.

Airline: buys oil futures to lock in jet fuel costs. Southwest had a structural cost advantage for years doing this. US exporter: sells EUR/USD forwards to lock in exchange rates on euro-denominated revenue. Floating rate borrower: enters an interest rate swap to convert variable debt to fixed cost.

Hull's key point

Hedging does not guarantee a better outcome than not hedging. If oil drops after the airline locks in $90, the hedge hurt them ex-post. That is not the point. The point is removing uncertainty so management can plan without a commodity bet embedded in the business.

Market Order
Execute immediately at best available price. Guaranteed fill, no price control. Slippage risk in thin markets.
Limit Order
Execute only at your specified price or better. Price control, no fill guarantee. Sits in the book until hit or cancelled.
Stop Order
Dormant until trigger hit, then fires a market order. Used to cut losses. Risk: fast markets fill well past the trigger.
Stop-Limit Order
Converts to limit order at trigger. More price control, less fill certainty. Gaps through your limit means no fill.
Market-if-Touched (MIT)
Opposite of a stop. Triggers at your level and fires a market order. Used to enter on pullbacks or take profits at a target.
Day / GTC / FOK
Day: expires end of session. GTC: live until cancelled. Fill-or-Kill: fill in full immediately or cancel entirely.
Section 2 — Options
Ch. 9-17  ·  14 questions

An options contract gives the buyer the right, not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a fixed price on or before a specific date. The buyer pays a premium upfront. The seller collects the premium and takes on the obligation.

Four variables: underlying (stock, index, ETF, currency), strike K (the price you can buy or sell at), expiration T (the date the contract dies), premium (what you pay, non-refundable).

A call gives the right to buy. You want the underlying to go up. A put gives the right to sell. You want it to go down.

The asymmetry is the whole point. The buyer has a right. The seller has an obligation. That asymmetry has a price and that price is the premium. One US equity options contract covers 100 shares.

Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value Call intrinsic = max(S_0 - K, 0) Put intrinsic = max(K - S_0, 0)

Intrinsic value is what the option is worth if exercised right now. Stock at $50, call K=$45: intrinsic = $5. Cannot go negative. OTM options have zero intrinsic value.

Time value is everything above intrinsic. Stock at $50, call K=$45 trading at $7: intrinsic=$5, time value=$2. The market pays $2 extra because there is still time for the stock to move further.

Time value bleeds to zero as expiry approaches. ATM options have maximum time value because they sit on the knife edge where any move matters. On expiry day, premium equals intrinsic value exactly. OTM at expiry is worth zero.

Practical implication

When you buy options you pay for both components. You need the stock to move enough to cover the time value you paid or you lose money even if you called the direction right.

When you buy an option, there is no margin. You paid the premium upfront. That is your max loss. Nothing more to collect.

Margin only applies when you write (sell) options. You have an open-ended obligation. For a naked short call, margin is the greater of:

Option A: Premium received + 20% of stock price - OTM amount Option B: Premium received + 10% of stock price Example: stock $50, write call K=$55, premium=$2 A: $2 + (0.20*$50) - $5 = $7 per share = $700/contract B: $2 + (0.10*$50) = $7 per share

Key difference from futures margin: in futures both sides post margin because both have unlimited loss potential. In options only the short side posts margin. The long side already paid the premium which caps their loss.

Covered calls get much lower margin treatment. If you already own the shares, your stock is the collateral and broker margin drops dramatically or disappears.

See the interactive diagrams at options-visualizer.html. Set S0=$50, K=$50, Premium=$5 on each tab to compare directly.

Call: right to buy at K. Profit when S_T > K + premium. Loss capped at premium below breakeven.

Long Call: P&L = max(S_T - K, 0) - C Breakeven = K + C Max loss = C (premium paid) Max profit = unlimited

Put: right to sell at K. Profit when S_T < K - premium. Loss capped at premium above breakeven.

Long Put: P&L = max(K - S_T, 0) - P Breakeven = K - P Max loss = P (premium paid) Max profit = K - P (stock goes to zero)

Calls pay when the stock goes up. Puts pay when it goes down. Both have limited downside (the premium) and asymmetric upside. Switch to Short Call and Short Put on the visualizer to see the mirror image: the writer's payoff is the exact inverse.

Exercising means invoking your right. For a call you buy 100 shares at K. For a put you sell 100 shares at K. The writer is obligated to perform. Most brokers auto-exercise any option $0.01 or more ITM at expiry.

Exercise a call when: S_T > K Exercise a put when: S_T < K OTM at expiry: let it expire worthless.

American options can be exercised any time before expiry. Most US equity options are American. European options can only be exercised on expiry day. Most index options (SPX) are European.

Never exercise American calls early on non-dividend-paying stocks. Exercising kills the time value. Sell the option in the market instead and collect both intrinsic and time value. Only exception: exercise early to capture a large upcoming dividend.

Early exercise of puts can make sense. If the stock crashes near zero, the put is deep ITM and the max payout is K. Collect K now. K today is worth more than K a year from now. Exercise when interest earned on K outweighs remaining time value.

Covered Call: own 100 shares + write a call against them. Collect premium, cap your upside at K.

Position = Long Stock + Short Call Max profit = (K - S_0) + premium Max loss = S_0 - premium (stock goes to zero) Breakeven = S_0 - premium

Stock at $50, sell call K=$55, collect $3. Stock stays below $55: keep $3. Stock rips to $70: called away at $55, make $5 + $3 = $8, miss the move above $55. Use when you think the stock goes sideways or up modestly and want income on a position you already hold.

Protective Put: own 100 shares + buy a put. Pay premium, floor your downside at K.

Position = Long Stock + Long Put Max loss = (S_0 - K) + premium Max profit = unlimited minus premium paid Breakeven = S_0 + premium

Stock at $50, buy put K=$47, pay $2. Stock crashes to $30: exercise put, sell at $47, max loss = $5. Stock runs to $70: make $20 minus the $2 premium. Insurance. Use before a catalyst or to protect a large unrealized gain.

Covered calls generate income, sacrifice upside. Protective puts cost money, eliminate catastrophic downside.

A no-arbitrage relationship between a European call and put with the same strike, expiry, and underlying.

c + K*e^(-rT) = p + S_0

Two portfolios that pay max(S_T, K) at expiry must cost the same today. Portfolio A: long call + cash K*e^(-rT). Portfolio B: long put + long stock. Same payoff at every outcome, so same price now.

Example: S_0=$50, K=$50, r=5%, T=1yr, c=$4 K*e^(-rT) = 50*e^(-0.05) = $47.56 p = c + K*e^(-rT) - S_0 = $4 + $47.56 - $50 = $1.56 If put trades at $2: buy call + invest $47.56 + short put + short stock. Zero cost today, guaranteed profit at expiry. Arbitrage closes the gap.

Only holds for European options. For American options Hull gives bounds: S_0 - K <= C - P <= S_0 - K*e^(-rT).

Also holds with dividends: replace S_0 with S_0 minus PV of dividends.

Volatility and time move both calls and puts in the same direction. Everything else moves them in opposite directions. Vol is the most watched input on any options desk because it drives both sides simultaneously.

Buy one option, sell another of the same type in the same expiry. The sale offsets cost. You give up unlimited upside for a cheaper trade with defined risk and defined reward.

Bull Call Spread: buy lower-strike call (K1), sell higher-strike call (K2).

Example: stock $50, buy call K1=$50 at $4, sell call K2=$55 at $1.50 Net cost = $2.50 Max loss = $2.50 (stock stays below $50) Breakeven = $52.50 Max profit = ($55-$50) - $2.50 = $2.50 (stock above $55)

Bear Put Spread: buy higher-strike put (K2), sell lower-strike put (K1). Mirror image, used when you think the stock goes down moderately.

Example: stock $50, buy put K2=$50 at $3, sell put K1=$45 at $1 Net cost = $2.00 Max loss = $2.00 (stock stays above $50) Breakeven = $48.00 Max profit = ($50-$45) - $2 = $3 (stock below $45)

Use spreads when your view is directional but measured. Lower cost, lower breakeven, capped upside.

Three strikes, four legs. A bet that the stock stays near K2 at expiry. Buy one call at K1, sell two calls at K2, buy one call at K3. Strikes equally spaced.

Example: stock $50, K1=$45, K2=$50, K3=$55 Buy 1x Call K1=$45 at $6.00 Sell 2x Call K2=$50 at $3.00 (collect $6.00) Buy 1x Call K3=$55 at $1.00 Net cost = $1.00 Max profit = (K2-K1) - premium = $5 - $1 = $4 (stock closes at $50) Max loss = $1.00 (stock closes below $45 or above $55) Breakevens = $46 and $54

The long wings (K1 and K3) cap your max loss. Without them you have a naked short straddle with unlimited downside. With them you lose $1 no matter what.

Use when you think the stock is going nowhere. Post-catalyst, vol is elevated, you want to short volatility with defined downside. Maximum payout if the stock closes exactly at K2.

Can also be built with puts (same payoff by put-call parity) or as an iron butterfly: sell a call and put at K2, buy a call at K3, buy a put at K1. Collects net premium upfront instead of paying it. More capital efficient.

Buy a call and a put at the same strike and expiry. Zero directional view. You just think the stock moves a lot.

Example: stock $50, buy call K=$50 at $3, buy put K=$50 at $2.50 Total premium = $5.50 Max loss = $5.50 (stock closes exactly at $50) Upside BE = $55.50 Downside BE = $44.50 Stock at $60: call pays $10, net = $10 - $5.50 = +$4.50 Stock at $38: put pays $12, net = $12 - $5.50 = +$6.50 Stock at $50: both worthless, lose $5.50

Classic pre-earnings play. You know a catalyst is coming but not the direction. You are betting the actual move exceeds what the market has priced in.

Vol crush risk

Market makers jack up implied vol before events. After the event vol collapses instantly even if the stock moved. You can be right on the magnitude and still lose because the vol you bought deflated faster than the intrinsic gain you collected. This is called vol crush.

A strangle is the cheaper version: buy OTM call and OTM put instead. Lower premium, wider breakevens, needs a bigger move to pay off.

Two models. Binomial trees for intuition. Black-Scholes-Merton for closed-form pricing.

Binomial tree: break time into steps. At each step stock goes up by u or down by d. Work backwards from expiry using risk-neutral probabilities. As steps increase, converges to BSM.

Risk-neutral up probability: p = (e^(rT) - d) / (u - d)

Black-Scholes-Merton: assumes geometric Brownian motion. Build a riskless portfolio of option + underlying in the right ratio. Solve the resulting differential equation.

c = S_0*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) p = K*e^(-rT)*N(-d2) - S_0*N(-d1) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T)) d2 = d1 - sigma*sqrt(T) Hull example: S_0=$42, K=$40, r=10%, sigma=20%, T=0.5 d1=0.7693, d2=0.6278 -> c=$4.76, p=$0.81

Four inputs are known (S_0, K, T, r). Sigma is not. Traders back it out from market prices: the sigma that makes BSM equal the market price is implied volatility. Traders price options using implied vol, not historical vol. The market is always forward-looking.

The volatility number you back out of an option's market price using BSM. The market's forward-looking estimate of how much the stock will move over the life of the option.

Market Price + BSM formula -> solve for sigma -> Implied Vol

If you think the stock will move more than implied vol suggests, buy options. You are buying vol cheap. If you think it will move less, sell options. You are selling vol rich. The vol view is the trade.

VIX is the implied vol of 30-day S&P 500 options. Normal range 10-20. Hit 80 in October 2008 after Lehman. Spiked again in March 2020.

Vol skew: BSM assumes vol is constant across strikes. The market disagrees. For equities, OTM puts trade at higher implied vol than OTM calls. The market prices in crash risk. Traders pay up for downside protection.

Vol Skew (equity): Vol Smile (FX): IV IV | * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * * | * * |______________> Strike |______________> Strike OTM put ATM OTM call OTM put ATM OTM call

Vol crush: implied vol spikes before known events (earnings, Fed). The moment the event passes, vol collapses even if the stock moved. Buying straddles into earnings can lose money because the vol deflation outweighs the intrinsic gain.

Same option mechanics. Different underlying and different BSM adjustment.

Index options (S&P 500, Nasdaq 100): the index pays continuous dividends across its constituents. Substitute dividend yield q into BSM.

c = S_0*e^(-qT)*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r - q + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T))

Cash settled (you cannot deliver 500 stocks). Most major index options are European (SPX). SPY options on the ETF are American. Used by portfolio managers to hedge entire equity books in one trade.

Currency options (EUR/USD, GBP/USD): the foreign currency earns interest at rate rf. Substitute rf in place of q. This is the Garman-Kohlhagen model.

c = S_0*e^(-rf*T)*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r - rf + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T))

S_0 is the spot exchange rate. Trade both on exchanges (PHLX) and heavily OTC because corporations need customized sizes and dates. A US company receiving EUR 10M in six months buys a EUR put to lock in the exchange rate while keeping the ability to benefit if the dollar weakens.
