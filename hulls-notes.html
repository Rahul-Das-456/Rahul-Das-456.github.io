Section 1 — Forwards and Futures
Ch. 1-5  ·  12 questions

Both are agreements to buy or sell an asset at a future date for a price locked in today. Same economic idea. Different wrappers.

A forward is a private OTC deal. Fully customizable: any size, any date, any underlying. No exchange, no clearinghouse. You are exposed to the other side.

A future is the standardized exchange-traded version (CME, ICE, CBOE). No customization. The clearinghouse steps between both sides and eliminates counterparty risk.

Forwards settle once at maturity. Futures are marked to market every day. Gains and losses hit your margin account overnight.

Perfectly linear. No kinks, no caps, no floors. Same shape for both forwards and futures.

Long (buy at F_0): Short (sell at F_0): P&L P&L | / | \ + | / + | \ | / | \ ---|---------/------> S_T ------|---------\----> S_T | / F_0 | F_0 \ - | / - | \
Long: P&L = S_T - F_0 Short: P&L = F_0 - S_T

No premium paid upfront. Zero cost to enter. Both sides fully obligated.

Counterparty risk. Delivery day comes, the market moved against the other side, and they do not show up. No exchange, no clearinghouse, nobody guaranteeing settlement.

Two additional risks: liquidity risk (no exchange to exit early, you negotiate a termination) and operational risk (bilateral customized contracts have more room for errors).

Futures eliminate counterparty risk. You trade customization for certainty of settlement.

The clearinghouse sits between every futures trade. Once your trade executes, it becomes the buyer to every seller and seller to every buyer. Counterparty risk is centralized onto one entity built to handle it.

Three mechanisms: margin requirements (collateral posted upfront), mark to market (losses collected daily, no giant end-of-term exposure), default fund (pooled contributions from clearing members absorb defaults before the clearinghouse's own capital is touched).

2008 context

Lehman had massive bilateral OTC exposure with no clearinghouse. When they failed nobody knew who owed what to whom. Exchange-cleared futures kept functioning normally through the same period. Dodd-Frank 2010 pushed large portions of OTC derivatives into central clearing because of this.

At expiry, none. Identical formula, identical shape, identical breakeven at F_0.

Theoretical difference: if asset prices are positively correlated with interest rates, futures prices will be slightly higher than forward prices. Daily gains get reinvested at higher rates; daily losses are paid when rates are lower. The asymmetry gets priced in. Negligible for most assets.

Practical difference: path dependency. A forward ignores what happens between today and maturity. Futures get margin-called along the way. You can call the direction right and still get wiped out if you could not survive the drawdown to your margin floor.

Margin is a good-faith deposit to open a futures position. Not a fee, not a down payment. Pure collateral.

Initial margin: what you post to open (5-10% of notional). Maintenance margin: the floor (~75% of initial). Variation margin: daily cash that flows in and out via mark to market.

Gold: F_0=$1,750, contract=100oz, notional=$175,000 Initial=$6,000 | Maintenance=$4,500 Day 2: gold drops $25 -> lose $2,500 -> account $3,500 (ok) Day 3: gold drops $15 -> lose $1,500 -> account $2,000 (MARGIN CALL) Must top back up to $6,000 (initial), not $4,500 (maintenance). Wire $4,000 by EOD or position gets liquidated at market.
What people get wrong

You can nail the direction and still lose. Get margin-called at the bottom, cannot fund it, get liquidated. Then the asset recovers. Being right is not enough if you cannot survive the path.

The cost of carry model. Futures price = spot price plus net cost of carrying the asset to delivery. Arbitrage locks it in.

Base: F_0 = S_0 * e^(rT) Dividend-paying stock: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r-q)T) [q = dividend yield] Foreign currency: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r-rf)T) [rf = foreign rate] Commodity with storage: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r+u)T) [u = storage cost] With convenience yield: F_0 = S_0 * e^((r+u-y)T) [y = convenience yield]

If futures trade above the formula, you borrow cash, buy spot, sell futures, deliver at expiry, and pocket the spread risk-free. If too cheap, reverse it. Free money closes the gap instantly.

The benefit of physically holding a commodity that you cannot get from holding a futures contract. You own a refinery, you need crude on hand. If supply gets disrupted tomorrow, a paper contract cannot feed your refinery. That operational value is the convenience yield.

It reduces the futures price. Higher convenience yield means lower F_0 relative to spot. When y is high, people bid up spot. Futures stays lower because the benefit does not transfer to paper.

You cannot observe it directly. Back it out from market prices using the pricing equation. Matters most for oil, gas, and agricultural products. Financial assets have no convenience yield. High convenience yield drives backwardation.

Contango: futures above spot, longer-dated contracts more expensive than shorter-dated. Curve slopes upward. Normal state. Cost of carry doing its job.

Backwardation: futures below spot, near-dated contracts more expensive than far-dated. Curve slopes downward. Convenience yield overwhelms cost of carry. Market says the asset is more valuable in your hands today than in storage for future delivery.

Contango: Backwardation: Price Price | * | * (spot) | * | * | * | * | * (spot) | * |____________________> |____________________> Time to delivery Time to delivery

Roll yield: in contango you sell cheap and buy expensive on every roll, bleeding steadily. In backwardation you collect on every roll. This is why commodity ETFs holding futures can massively underperform spot in sustained contango.

To reduce uncertainty around cash flows. Not to make money. To remove financial variables they cannot control so they can focus on the business they actually run.

Airline: buys oil futures to lock in jet fuel costs. Southwest had a structural cost advantage for years doing this. US exporter: sells EUR/USD forwards to lock in exchange rates on euro-denominated revenue. Floating rate borrower: enters an interest rate swap to convert variable debt to fixed cost.

Hull's key point

Hedging does not guarantee a better outcome than not hedging. If oil drops after the airline locks in $90, the hedge hurt them ex-post. That is not the point. The point is removing uncertainty so management can plan without a commodity bet embedded in the business.

Market Order
Execute immediately at best available price. Guaranteed fill, no price control. Slippage risk in thin markets.
Limit Order
Execute only at your specified price or better. Price control, no fill guarantee. Sits in the book until hit or cancelled.
Stop Order
Dormant until trigger hit, then fires a market order. Used to cut losses. Risk: fast markets fill well past the trigger.
Stop-Limit Order
Converts to limit order at trigger. More price control, less fill certainty. Gaps through your limit means no fill.
Market-if-Touched (MIT)
Opposite of a stop. Triggers at your level and fires a market order. Used to enter on pullbacks or take profits at a target.
Day / GTC / FOK
Day: expires end of session. GTC: live until cancelled. Fill-or-Kill: fill in full immediately or cancel entirely.

Every option premium breaks into two components. Intrinsic value is what you could collect right now if you exercised immediately. Time value is everything the market pays on top of that for the chance the option moves further ITM before expiry.

Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value Call intrinsic = max(S_0 - K, 0) Put intrinsic = max(K - S_0, 0) Time value = Premium - Intrinsic Value

Intrinsic can never go negative. If a call is OTM, intrinsic is zero, not negative. You do not exercise it and the floor holds at zero.

ITM Call: Stock $185, K=$180, Premium=$9 Intrinsic = max(185 - 180, 0) = $5.00 Time Value = $9.00 - $5.00 = $4.00 OTM Put: Stock $185, K=$175, Premium=$3 Intrinsic = max(175 - 185, 0) = $0.00 Time Value = $3.00 - $0.00 = $3.00 (pure time value) ITM Put: Stock $185, K=$200, Premium=$18 Intrinsic = max(200 - 185, 0) = $15.00 Time Value = $18.00 - $15.00 = $3.00 ATM Call: Stock $50, K=$50, Premium=$4 Intrinsic = max(50 - 50, 0) = $0.00 Time Value = $4.00 - $0.00 = $4.00 (maximum time value)

ATM options carry the most time value. The stock sits right on the knife edge and any move matters, so the market charges the most for that optionality. Deep ITM options are almost entirely intrinsic with minimal time value. Deep OTM options are pure time value with zero intrinsic.

Time value decays to zero as expiry approaches. This is theta working in real time. On expiry day, premium equals intrinsic exactly. If OTM at expiry, the option is worth zero.

Why it matters in practice

When you buy options you pay both components. The stock has to move enough to cover the time value you paid or you lose money even if you got the direction right. That gap between intrinsic gain and time value paid is what kills most long option trades.

Section 2 — Options
Ch. 9-17  ·  14 questions

An options contract gives the buyer the right, not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a fixed price on or before a specific date. The buyer pays a premium upfront. The seller collects the premium and takes on the obligation.

Four variables: underlying (stock, index, ETF, currency), strike K (the price you can buy or sell at), expiration T (the date the contract dies), premium (what you pay, non-refundable).

A call gives the right to buy. You want the underlying to go up. A put gives the right to sell. You want it to go down.

The asymmetry is the whole point. The buyer has a right. The seller has an obligation. That asymmetry has a price and that price is the premium. One US equity options contract covers 100 shares.

Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value Call intrinsic = max(S_0 - K, 0) Put intrinsic = max(K - S_0, 0)

Intrinsic value is what the option is worth if exercised right now. Stock at $50, call K=$45: intrinsic = $5. Cannot go negative. OTM options have zero intrinsic value.

Time value is everything above intrinsic. Stock at $50, call K=$45 trading at $7: intrinsic=$5, time value=$2. The market pays $2 extra because there is still time for the stock to move further.

Time value bleeds to zero as expiry approaches. ATM options have maximum time value because they sit on the knife edge where any move matters. On expiry day, premium equals intrinsic value exactly. OTM at expiry is worth zero.

Practical implication

When you buy options you pay for both components. You need the stock to move enough to cover the time value you paid or you lose money even if you called the direction right.

When you buy an option, there is no margin. You paid the premium upfront. That is your max loss. Nothing more to collect.

Margin only applies when you write (sell) options. You have an open-ended obligation. For a naked short call, margin is the greater of:

Option A: Premium received + 20% of stock price - OTM amount Option B: Premium received + 10% of stock price Example: stock $50, write call K=$55, premium=$2 A: $2 + (0.20*$50) - $5 = $7 per share = $700/contract B: $2 + (0.10*$50) = $7 per share

Key difference from futures margin: in futures both sides post margin because both have unlimited loss potential. In options only the short side posts margin. The long side already paid the premium which caps their loss.

Covered calls get much lower margin treatment. If you already own the shares, your stock is the collateral and broker margin drops dramatically or disappears.

See the interactive diagrams at options-visualizer.html. Set S0=$50, K=$50, Premium=$5 on each tab to compare directly.

Call: right to buy at K. Profit when S_T > K + premium. Loss capped at premium below breakeven.

Long Call: P&L = max(S_T - K, 0) - C Breakeven = K + C Max loss = C (premium paid) Max profit = unlimited

Put: right to sell at K. Profit when S_T < K - premium. Loss capped at premium above breakeven.

Long Put: P&L = max(K - S_T, 0) - P Breakeven = K - P Max loss = P (premium paid) Max profit = K - P (stock goes to zero)

Calls pay when the stock goes up. Puts pay when it goes down. Both have limited downside (the premium) and asymmetric upside. Switch to Short Call and Short Put on the visualizer to see the mirror image: the writer's payoff is the exact inverse.

Exercising means invoking your right. For a call you buy 100 shares at K. For a put you sell 100 shares at K. The writer is obligated to perform. Most brokers auto-exercise any option $0.01 or more ITM at expiry.

Exercise a call when: S_T > K Exercise a put when: S_T < K OTM at expiry: let it expire worthless.

American options can be exercised any time before expiry. Most US equity options are American. European options can only be exercised on expiry day. Most index options (SPX) are European.

Never exercise American calls early on non-dividend-paying stocks. Exercising kills the time value. Sell the option in the market instead and collect both intrinsic and time value. Only exception: exercise early to capture a large upcoming dividend.

Early exercise of puts can make sense. If the stock crashes near zero, the put is deep ITM and the max payout is K. Collect K now. K today is worth more than K a year from now. Exercise when interest earned on K outweighs remaining time value.

Covered Call: own 100 shares + write a call against them. Collect premium, cap your upside at K.

Position = Long Stock + Short Call Max profit = (K - S_0) + premium Max loss = S_0 - premium (stock goes to zero) Breakeven = S_0 - premium

Stock at $50, sell call K=$55, collect $3. Stock stays below $55: keep $3. Stock rips to $70: called away at $55, make $5 + $3 = $8, miss the move above $55. Use when you think the stock goes sideways or up modestly and want income on a position you already hold.

Protective Put: own 100 shares + buy a put. Pay premium, floor your downside at K.

Position = Long Stock + Long Put Max loss = (S_0 - K) + premium Max profit = unlimited minus premium paid Breakeven = S_0 + premium

Stock at $50, buy put K=$47, pay $2. Stock crashes to $30: exercise put, sell at $47, max loss = $5. Stock runs to $70: make $20 minus the $2 premium. Insurance. Use before a catalyst or to protect a large unrealized gain.

Covered calls generate income, sacrifice upside. Protective puts cost money, eliminate catastrophic downside.

A no-arbitrage relationship between a European call and put with the same strike, expiry, and underlying.

c + K*e^(-rT) = p + S_0

Two portfolios that pay max(S_T, K) at expiry must cost the same today. Portfolio A: long call + cash K*e^(-rT). Portfolio B: long put + long stock. Same payoff at every outcome, so same price now.

Example: S_0=$50, K=$50, r=5%, T=1yr, c=$4 K*e^(-rT) = 50*e^(-0.05) = $47.56 p = c + K*e^(-rT) - S_0 = $4 + $47.56 - $50 = $1.56 If put trades at $2: buy call + invest $47.56 + short put + short stock. Zero cost today, guaranteed profit at expiry. Arbitrage closes the gap.

Only holds for European options. For American options Hull gives bounds: S_0 - K <= C - P <= S_0 - K*e^(-rT).

Also holds with dividends: replace S_0 with S_0 minus PV of dividends.

Volatility and time move both calls and puts in the same direction. Everything else moves them in opposite directions. Vol is the most watched input on any options desk because it drives both sides simultaneously.

Buy one option, sell another of the same type in the same expiry. The sale offsets cost. You give up unlimited upside for a cheaper trade with defined risk and defined reward.

Bull Call Spread: buy lower-strike call (K1), sell higher-strike call (K2).

Example: stock $50, buy call K1=$50 at $4, sell call K2=$55 at $1.50 Net cost = $2.50 Max loss = $2.50 (stock stays below $50) Breakeven = $52.50 Max profit = ($55-$50) - $2.50 = $2.50 (stock above $55)

Bear Put Spread: buy higher-strike put (K2), sell lower-strike put (K1). Mirror image, used when you think the stock goes down moderately.

Example: stock $50, buy put K2=$50 at $3, sell put K1=$45 at $1 Net cost = $2.00 Max loss = $2.00 (stock stays above $50) Breakeven = $48.00 Max profit = ($50-$45) - $2 = $3 (stock below $45)

Use spreads when your view is directional but measured. Lower cost, lower breakeven, capped upside.

Three strikes, four legs. A bet that the stock stays near K2 at expiry. Buy one call at K1, sell two calls at K2, buy one call at K3. Strikes equally spaced.

Example: stock $50, K1=$45, K2=$50, K3=$55 Buy 1x Call K1=$45 at $6.00 Sell 2x Call K2=$50 at $3.00 (collect $6.00) Buy 1x Call K3=$55 at $1.00 Net cost = $1.00 Max profit = (K2-K1) - premium = $5 - $1 = $4 (stock closes at $50) Max loss = $1.00 (stock closes below $45 or above $55) Breakevens = $46 and $54

The long wings (K1 and K3) cap your max loss. Without them you have a naked short straddle with unlimited downside. With them you lose $1 no matter what.

Use when you think the stock is going nowhere. Post-catalyst, vol is elevated, you want to short volatility with defined downside. Maximum payout if the stock closes exactly at K2.

Can also be built with puts (same payoff by put-call parity) or as an iron butterfly: sell a call and put at K2, buy a call at K3, buy a put at K1. Collects net premium upfront instead of paying it. More capital efficient.

Buy a call and a put at the same strike and expiry. Zero directional view. You just think the stock moves a lot.

Example: stock $50, buy call K=$50 at $3, buy put K=$50 at $2.50 Total premium = $5.50 Max loss = $5.50 (stock closes exactly at $50) Upside BE = $55.50 Downside BE = $44.50 Stock at $60: call pays $10, net = $10 - $5.50 = +$4.50 Stock at $38: put pays $12, net = $12 - $5.50 = +$6.50 Stock at $50: both worthless, lose $5.50

Classic pre-earnings play. You know a catalyst is coming but not the direction. You are betting the actual move exceeds what the market has priced in.

Vol crush risk

Market makers jack up implied vol before events. After the event vol collapses instantly even if the stock moved. You can be right on the magnitude and still lose because the vol you bought deflated faster than the intrinsic gain you collected. This is called vol crush.

A strangle is the cheaper version: buy OTM call and OTM put instead. Lower premium, wider breakevens, needs a bigger move to pay off.

Two models. Binomial trees for intuition. Black-Scholes-Merton for closed-form pricing.

Binomial tree: break time into steps. At each step stock goes up by u or down by d. Work backwards from expiry using risk-neutral probabilities. As steps increase, converges to BSM.

Risk-neutral up probability: p = (e^(rT) - d) / (u - d)

Black-Scholes-Merton: assumes geometric Brownian motion. Build a riskless portfolio of option + underlying in the right ratio. Solve the resulting differential equation.

c = S_0*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) p = K*e^(-rT)*N(-d2) - S_0*N(-d1) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T)) d2 = d1 - sigma*sqrt(T) Hull example: S_0=$42, K=$40, r=10%, sigma=20%, T=0.5 d1=0.7693, d2=0.6278 -> c=$4.76, p=$0.81

Four inputs are known (S_0, K, T, r). Sigma is not. Traders back it out from market prices: the sigma that makes BSM equal the market price is implied volatility. Traders price options using implied vol, not historical vol. The market is always forward-looking.

The volatility number you back out of an option's market price using BSM. The market's forward-looking estimate of how much the stock will move over the life of the option.

Market Price + BSM formula -> solve for sigma -> Implied Vol

If you think the stock will move more than implied vol suggests, buy options. You are buying vol cheap. If you think it will move less, sell options. You are selling vol rich. The vol view is the trade.

VIX is the implied vol of 30-day S&P 500 options. Normal range 10-20. Hit 80 in October 2008 after Lehman. Spiked again in March 2020.

Vol skew: BSM assumes vol is constant across strikes. The market disagrees. For equities, OTM puts trade at higher implied vol than OTM calls. The market prices in crash risk. Traders pay up for downside protection.

Vol Skew (equity): Vol Smile (FX): IV IV | * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * * | * * |______________> Strike |______________> Strike OTM put ATM OTM call OTM put ATM OTM call

Vol crush: implied vol spikes before known events (earnings, Fed). The moment the event passes, vol collapses even if the stock moved. Buying straddles into earnings can lose money because the vol deflation outweighs the intrinsic gain.

Same option mechanics. Different underlying and different BSM adjustment.

Index options (S&P 500, Nasdaq 100): the index pays continuous dividends across its constituents. Substitute dividend yield q into BSM.

c = S_0*e^(-qT)*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r - q + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T))

Cash settled (you cannot deliver 500 stocks). Most major index options are European (SPX). SPY options on the ETF are American. Used by portfolio managers to hedge entire equity books in one trade.

Currency options (EUR/USD, GBP/USD): the foreign currency earns interest at rate rf. Substitute rf in place of q. This is the Garman-Kohlhagen model.

c = S_0*e^(-rf*T)*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r - rf + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T))

S_0 is the spot exchange rate. Trade both on exchanges (PHLX) and heavily OTC because corporations need customized sizes and dates. A US company receiving EUR 10M in six months buys a EUR put to lock in the exchange rate while keeping the ability to benefit if the dollar weakens.

Section 3 — Pricing Models
Ch. 11-15  ·  6 questions

A binomial tree models stock price movement in discrete steps. At each step the stock either goes up by factor u or down by factor d. You build the tree forward, calculate option payoffs at expiry, then work backwards to get today's price.

One-step setup: Stock today: S_0 Up node: S_0 * u -> option payoff f_u = max(S_0*u - K, 0) Down node: S_0 * d -> option payoff f_d = max(S_0*d - K, 0) Risk-neutral probability of up move: p = (e^(rT) - d) / (u - d) Option price today: f = e^(-rT) * [p*f_u + (1-p)*f_d]

The risk-neutral probability p is not a real-world probability. It is the probability that makes the stock earn the risk-free rate. You price all derivatives in a world where every asset earns r.

Hull example (Ch. 13)

S_0=$20, K=$21, u=1.1, d=0.9, r=12%, T=3mo. f_u=max(22-21,0)=$1, f_d=0. p=(e^(0.03)-0.9)/(1.1-0.9)=0.6523. f=e^(-0.03)*(0.6523*1+0.3477*0)=$0.633.

Break T into N steps of length dt = T/N. At each step the stock goes up or down. After N steps you have N+1 terminal nodes. Calculate option payoff at each terminal node, then fold backwards through the tree step by step.

Step size: dt = T/N Up factor: u = e^(sigma*sqrt(dt)) Down factor: d = 1/u Risk-neutral prob: p = (e^(r*dt) - d) / (u - d) At each node work backwards: f = e^(-r*dt) * [p*f_up + (1-p)*f_down]
2-step tree (each node branches up or down): S*u^2 -> payoff / S*u / S S*u*d -> payoff \ / S*d S*d^2 -> payoff

For American options, at each node check whether immediate exercise beats holding. Take the max of exercise value and hold value. This is why binomial trees handle American options directly while BSM requires adjustments.

As N increases, the binomial tree converges to Black-Scholes. Practitioners use 50-200 steps for accuracy. More steps, better precision, more computation.

At expiry: exercise when ITM, let expire when OTM. Brokers auto-exercise any option $0.01 or more ITM.

Exercise call when: S_T > K Exercise put when: S_T < K

American calls, no dividends: never exercise early. Exercising kills time value. Sell the option instead and collect intrinsic plus time value. Only exception: exercise early to capture a large upcoming dividend.

American puts: early exercise can make sense. If stock crashes near zero the put is deep ITM. Max payout is K. Collect K now rather than waiting. K today is worth more than K in the future.

The decision rule: exercise the put early when interest earned on K from now to expiry exceeds the remaining time value of the option.

Assumes stock prices follow geometric Brownian motion. Build a riskless portfolio of option plus underlying in the right ratio. That portfolio must earn the risk-free rate. Solve the differential equation for a closed-form price.

c = S_0*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) p = K*e^(-rT)*N(-d2) - S_0*N(-d1) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T)) d2 = d1 - sigma*sqrt(T) N(x) = cumulative standard normal CDF

Five inputs: S_0 (observable), K (contract), T (contract), r (T-bills/SOFR), sigma (not observable, backed out as implied vol).

What each piece means: N(d2) is the risk-neutral probability the call finishes ITM. K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) is the PV of what you pay if exercised. S_0*N(d1) is the expected stock value received, probability-weighted. N(d1) is also delta.

Hull example: S_0=$42, K=$40, r=10%, sigma=20%, T=0.5 d1=0.7693, d2=0.6278 c = 42*0.7791 - 40*e^(-0.05)*0.7349 = $4.76 p = $0.81

BSM assumes constant vol, continuous trading, no dividends, no transaction costs. All violated in practice. Traders use it anyway as a common language for quoting options in vol terms.

The sigma you back out of an observed market price using BSM. The market's forward-looking estimate of how much the stock will move.

Take market price + plug in S_0, K, T, r -> solve for sigma -> implied vol

Four BSM inputs are known. Sigma is not. Traders iterate numerically until BSM output equals the market price. That sigma is the trade.

If you think realized vol will exceed implied vol, buy options. If you think realized vol will fall short, sell options. The directional view is secondary.

VIX is 30-day implied vol on S&P 500 options. Normal range 10-20. Spiked to 80 in Oct 2008 and March 2020.

Vol crush: implied vol spikes before known events. After the event it collapses instantly even if the stock moved. Buying straddles into earnings loses money when vol deflation outweighs intrinsic gains.

Simulate thousands of random stock price paths using geometric Brownian motion. Calculate the option payoff on each path. Average all the payoffs and discount back to today. That average is the option price.

One path, one time step: S_T = S_0 * exp((r - sigma^2/2)*T + sigma*sqrt(T)*z) z = random draw from standard normal N(0,1) Repeat 10,000+ times: -> calculate payoff on each path -> average all payoffs -> discount: price = e^(-rT) * average payoff

Where Monte Carlo beats BSM and binomial trees: path-dependent options where the payoff depends on the full price history, not just the final price.

Asian option: payoff based on average stock price over the life of the option. BSM cannot handle this. Monte Carlo tracks every step and averages.

Barrier option: expires worthless (knock-out) or activates (knock-in) if the stock hits a barrier at any point. Monte Carlo checks the barrier at every simulated step.

Downside: slow. More paths means more accuracy but more computation. Standard error shrinks as 1/sqrt(N). To halve the error you need four times as many paths. Variance reduction techniques (antithetic variates, control variates) speed convergence.

Section 4 — Greeks and Hedging
Ch. 18-19  ·  5 questions

Greeks measure how sensitive an option price is to each input. Every derivatives desk manages these daily.

Delta (d): dC/dS — sensitivity to stock price Gamma (G): d2C/dS2 — rate of change of delta Theta (Th): dC/dt — sensitivity to time passing Vega (v): dC/dsigma — sensitivity to volatility Rho (r): dC/dr — sensitivity to interest rates

Delta: call delta = N(d1), ranges 0 to 1. Put delta = N(d1)-1, ranges -1 to 0. ATM options delta ~0.5. Deep ITM call delta approaches 1 and behaves like stock. OTM call delta approaches 0. A delta-0.6 call gains $0.60 for every $1 rise in stock.

Gamma: N'(d1)/(S_0*sigma*sqrt(T)). Always positive for long options. Highest for ATM options near expiry. High gamma means delta is shifting fast and you must rebalance constantly. Cannot be hedged with stock alone, needs other options.

Theta: usually negative. Options bleed value as time passes. ATM near-expiry options decay fastest. The theta-gamma relationship: theta + 0.5*sigma^2*S^2*gamma = r*portfolio. High gamma, high theta. That is the core tradeoff.

Vega: S_0*sqrt(T)*N'(d1). Always positive for long options. Higher vol makes both calls and puts more expensive. Cannot be hedged with stock, needs other options. One percent rise in implied vol changes option value by approximately vega*0.01.

Rho: K*T*e^(-rT)*N(d2) for calls. Positive for calls, negative for puts. Smallest Greek for short-dated options. Matters more for LEAPS.

A neutral portfolio has zero sensitivity to a specific risk factor. You are removing a specific Greek from your exposure.

Delta neutral: combine option position with the underlying in a ratio that makes total delta zero. Sold 100 calls with delta 0.6: buy 60 shares. Net delta = -60 + 60 = 0. Small stock moves cancel out.

Shares needed to delta hedge = delta * number of options Example: short 100 calls, delta=0.6 -> buy 60 shares

Gamma neutral: delta neutrality does not protect against large moves. Gamma captures how fast delta is shifting. To neutralize gamma you need another option position since stock has zero gamma.

If portfolio gamma = G_P, and a traded option has gamma G_T: Add w_T = -G_P / G_T units of the traded option. Then re-establish delta neutrality with stock.

Vega neutral: same logic. Portfolio vega + (w_T * option vega) = 0. Use another option to zero it out, then rebalance delta with stock.

In practice traders do not zero everything simultaneously. They stay within risk limits on each Greek. The goal is staying inside bounds, not perfect neutrality, because the cost of hedging every Greek every minute would eat all the profit.

Delta changes constantly as the stock moves. Dynamic hedging means continuously rebalancing the stock position to maintain delta neutrality over the life of the option.

Hull's bank example (Ch. 19)

Bank sells 100,000 calls. S_0=$49, K=$50, T=20wks, sigma=20%, r=5%. BSM price=$240k. Delta=0.522, buy 52,200 shares, borrow $2.56M. Week 1: stock falls to $48.12, delta drops to 0.458, sell 6,400 shares, reduce borrowing. Week 3: stock jumps to $50.25, delta rises to 0.596, buy more shares. Repeat weekly for 20 weeks. Final hedging cost ~$263k vs BSM price $240k. Gap exists because rebalancing is weekly not continuous and vol is not perfectly constant.

As rebalancing frequency increases, cost converges to the BSM price. Continuous rebalancing in theory perfectly replicates the option payoff. In practice transaction costs make continuous rebalancing prohibitive. Real desks rebalance daily or when delta drifts past a threshold.

The key insight: BSM assumes you can continuously delta hedge. The BSM price is literally the cost of running that hedge to replication. If you sell an option and hedge it perfectly, your net cost equals the premium you collected. Your profit comes from the bid-ask spread and from selling options when implied vol exceeds realized vol.

Volatility trading means taking a view on whether implied vol is too high or too low relative to what you expect realized vol to be. Direction is not the point. Vol is.

Long vol (buying volatility): you think the stock will move more than implied vol suggests. Buy a straddle or strangle. Delta hedge away the directional exposure immediately. Now you are long gamma and long vega. You make money if realized vol exceeds implied vol. You lose money from theta every day the stock sits still.

Short vol (selling volatility): you think the stock will move less than implied vol suggests. Sell a straddle or strangle. Delta hedge. Now you are short gamma and short vega. You collect theta daily but face losses if the stock makes a big move.

Long vol trade: Buy straddle -> delta hedge -> long gamma, long vega, short theta Profit if realized vol > implied vol Short vol trade: Sell straddle -> delta hedge -> short gamma, short vega, long theta Profit if realized vol < implied vol

The gamma-theta tradeoff is the heart of it. Long gamma positions profit from large moves but pay theta daily. Short gamma positions collect theta daily but bleed on large moves. Volatility trading is just choosing which side of that tradeoff you want based on your vol forecast.

Vol arb exploits the difference between implied volatility (what the market prices into options) and realized volatility (how much the stock actually moves). You strip out the directional exposure with delta hedging and isolate the vol bet.

The trade: if implied vol is 30% but you forecast realized vol of 20%, sell options and delta hedge. You collect the 30% vol premium. As you rebalance daily, your hedging cost reflects actual realized vol. The spread between what you sold at and what it cost to hedge is your profit.

P&L from vol arb (simplified): Daily P&L ~ 0.5 * Gamma * S^2 * (sigma_realized^2 - sigma_implied^2) * dt sigma_realized > sigma_implied -> long vol wins sigma_realized < sigma_implied -> short vol wins

In practice: buy a call, delta hedge it continuously. Your total cost to hedge is driven by realized vol. You paid implied vol for the option. If implied vol > realized vol, you overpaid and lose. If implied vol < realized vol, you got a cheap option and profit from delta hedging.

Risk: realized vol is unknown until expiry. You forecast it. You can be wrong. Also, large sudden moves (gap risk) kill short vol positions before you can rebalance. Vol arb is not risk-free. It is a systematic vol forecast being executed through options and delta hedging.

Section 5 — Volatility and Real Options
Ch. 17, 20, 34  ·  4 questions

Same option mechanics, different underlying, different BSM adjustment.

Index options: underlying is a stock index (S&P 500, Nasdaq). The index pays continuous dividends across its constituents. Substitute dividend yield q for r in BSM.

c = S_0*e^(-qT)*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r - q + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T))

Cash settled only (you cannot deliver 500 stocks). SPX options are European. SPY ETF options are American. Used by portfolio managers to hedge entire equity books in one trade.

Currency options: underlying is an exchange rate. Foreign currency earns interest at rate rf. Substitute rf for q. This is the Garman-Kohlhagen model.

c = S_0*e^(-rf*T)*N(d1) - K*e^(-rT)*N(d2) d1 = [ln(S_0/K) + (r - rf + sigma^2/2)*T] / (sigma*sqrt(T))

S_0 is spot exchange rate. r is domestic rate. rf is foreign rate. Heavy OTC market because corporates need custom sizes and dates. Used by multinationals to hedge FX revenue exposure while retaining upside if rates move favorably.

Index Currency Underlying Stock index Exchange rate BSM adjustment Div yield q Foreign rate rf Settlement Cash Cash or physical Main use Portfolio hedge FX risk mgmt

BSM assumes implied volatility is the same across all strikes. The market disagrees. Plot implied vol against strike for any single expiry and you get a curve. That curve is the vol smile or vol skew.

Equity skew: OTM puts have higher implied vol than OTM calls. Not symmetric. The market prices in crash risk. Investors pay up for downside protection, bidding up put implied vol. Called a skew, not a smile.

Equity vol skew: FX vol smile: IV IV | * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * | * * * | * * |______________> K |______________> K OTM put ATM OTM call OTM put ATM OTM call

FX smile: both OTM puts and OTM calls have elevated implied vol relative to ATM. Large moves in either direction are seen as roughly equally likely for currencies. More symmetric than equities.

Why it exists: three reasons. First, stock returns have fat tails: large moves happen more often than the normal distribution assumes, so OTM options are worth more than BSM suggests. Second, leverage effect: stocks tend to be more volatile when prices fall (higher debt-to-equity, more uncertainty). Third, supply and demand: portfolio managers systematically buy OTM puts for protection, bidding up their price.

The smile is direct evidence that BSM is wrong. Traders know this. They still use BSM as a quoting convention and manage the residual through the vol surface.

Term structure of volatility: implied vol varies across expiration dates, not just strikes. Plot ATM implied vol against time to expiry and you get the term structure.

Term structure shapes: Normal (upward): Inverted (downward): IV IV | * | * | * | * | * | * |* | * |________> Expiry |________> Expiry When current vol is low, market expects it to rise (upward sloping). When current vol is high, market expects mean reversion (inverted).

VIX typically shows an inverted term structure during market stress because near-term fear is priced higher than long-term uncertainty.

Volatility surface: combine the vol smile (across strikes) with the term structure (across expiries) and you get a 2D surface: implied vol as a function of both strike K and expiry T.

Vol surface (conceptual): IV /| / | / | <- near expiry: steep skew / | / | <- far expiry: flatter skew /_____|_____________> Strike K / Expiry T

Every options desk marks this surface daily. When the surface shifts, every single position re-prices simultaneously. Managing a book means managing exposure to surface movements, not just individual options. Options market makers quote in terms of where the surface sits and hedge their book against surface risk.

Real options apply options pricing theory to corporate investment decisions. A company has a choice, not an obligation, to take a business action. That flexibility has value just like a financial option does.

Standard NPV analysis ignores managerial flexibility. It assumes you commit fully upfront. Real options pricing captures the value of waiting, expanding, contracting, or abandoning depending on how conditions evolve.

Four main types: Option to expand: if project succeeds, invest more -> like a call option Option to abandon: if project fails, exit and recover salvage -> like a put option Option to defer: wait and invest only if conditions improve -> like a call Option to switch: change inputs or outputs as prices shift -> portfolio of options

Example: an oil company owns undeveloped reserves. They do not have to drill. If oil prices rise enough to make drilling profitable, they exercise and drill. If prices stay low they wait. That optionality has real economic value not captured by a simple DCF.

Real option value = NPV(invest now) + option value of waiting Underlying asset S = PV of project cash flows Strike K = investment cost Sigma = volatility of project cash flows T = time window to make the decision r = risk-free rate

Higher uncertainty (sigma) increases real option value just like it increases financial option value. This flips the standard intuition: more volatile projects can be worth more, not less, because you have the option to invest only when things look good and abandon when they do not.

Used in natural resources, pharma R&D, tech platform investment, real estate development, and any capital decision with staged commitments.

Delta
Δ (delta)

If the stock moves $1, how much does my option move?

Tap to reveal ↓
Delta explained

Delta tells you how closely your option tracks the stock. A delta of 0.6 means your call gains about $0.60 when the stock rises $1.

Calls have positive delta (0 to 1). Puts have negative delta (−1 to 0). An at-the-money option sits around ±0.50 — a coin flip on direction.

Call delta: 0 to +1 Put delta: −1 to 0 ATM delta: ≈ ±0.50

Deep in-the-money call delta → 1 (moves dollar for dollar with stock). Out-of-the-money → near 0 (barely reacts).

Gamma
Γ (gamma)

How fast does my delta change as the stock moves?

Tap to reveal ↓
Gamma explained

Delta doesn't stay fixed — it changes as the stock moves. Gamma measures how quickly delta shifts per $1 move in the stock.

Think of delta as your speedometer and gamma as your acceleration. A high-gamma option is one where your speed is changing fast — you need to re-hedge more often.

Gamma is highest: ATM options near expiry Gamma is lowest: deep ITM or OTM options

You can't hedge gamma with just stock — you need another option. High gamma = the option is very sensitive to big moves in either direction.

Theta
Θ (theta)

How much value does my option lose each day just from time passing?

Tap to reveal ↓
Theta explained

Options are like ice — they melt as time passes, even if nothing else changes. Theta is the daily melt rate.

If you bought a call with theta = −$0.05, you lose $5 per contract every single day just from the clock ticking, regardless of where the stock goes.

Theta is usually negative for option buyers Theta accelerates near expiry (ATM options)

Option sellers love theta — they're on the other side, collecting that daily decay. The tradeoff: sellers are short gamma and get hurt by big moves.

Vega
ν (vega)

How much does my option change when implied volatility moves 1%?

Tap to reveal ↓
Vega explained

Options are bets on movement. When the market expects more movement (higher implied vol), all options get more expensive — both calls and puts. Vega measures that sensitivity.

A vega of 0.20 means your option gains $0.20 in value for every 1% rise in implied volatility, regardless of which direction the stock moves.

Vega is always positive for option buyers Both calls and puts gain value when vol rises Vega is highest for ATM, long-dated options

Buying before earnings? You're buying high vega. After the event, vol collapses (vol crush) and your option loses value even if the stock moved.

Rho
ρ (rho)

How does my option change when interest rates go up 1%?

Tap to reveal ↓
Rho explained

Higher interest rates make calls slightly more valuable and puts slightly less valuable. The intuition: a call lets you defer paying for stock — that deferred cash earns more when rates are higher, so the option is worth more.

Calls: positive rho (rate rise → call worth more) Puts: negative rho (rate rise → put worth less)

Rho is the smallest and least-watched Greek for short-dated options. It matters most for long-dated options (LEAPS) or when central banks are actively moving rates.
